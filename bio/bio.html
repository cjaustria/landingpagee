The Apple Was Eaten

Christian Austria · Personal Biography

Childhood Teenage College

Childhood Years

My childhood feels blurry now, but I clearly remember my family calling me by my name, Christian, as if I were the center of their little universe.1 I was born in Cainta, Rizal in 2005, surrounded by a loving family who made me feel like the protagonist of a movie.2 I dreamed of becoming a priest, an astronaut, a scientist like Newton, or even a hacker.3 You would probably think I watched too many fictional movies, and honestly, you would be right.4 I had friends, but I was shy and introverted, rarely approaching anyone unless invited.5 I loved going to the palengke with my mother because I enjoyed hearing tindera and tindero calling me pogi, smelling the fresh fish, watching them move, seeing how someone would remove their organs, and exploring the colorful candies around the stalls.6 One of my subtle favorite things was reading any word I could see, and whenever I was outside, I would stare at cars passing by as if staring into a void that somehow felt satisfying.7 During rainy days, I was not allowed to play in the rain because I had a weak body, hence I spent many afternoons at my cousin's house watching Adventure Time, Regular Show, The Grim Adventures of Billy and Mandy, Thomas and Friends, and Frozen.8 Sometimes I wish I could be a child again, and I would sacrifice everything just to feel that innocence once more.9 One day at church, my friends and I had a surprisingly deep conversation inspired by the homily.10 We related the message to ourselves, and I asked, “Gusto mo na ba tumanda or bumalik sa pagkabata uli?” even though we were still kids then.11 Even as a child, I questioned everything like a mini-Socrates trying to understand life.12 I was the smart one, but I did not go all out in school because I never saw the point in competing for grades when learning was never supposed to be a race.13 I just wanted to study what I truly loved, not what was forced upon me.14 Around 2011, I experienced my first genuine emotion from deep within.15 I saw my siblings crying when we learned that our grandfather had died, and at first, I just stared.16 Someone whispered to me, questioning why I did not cry, reminding me not to follow the taboo that men should not show emotion.17 In that moment, I cried, understanding death, loss, and the truth that no one comes back once gone.18 I also remember meeting Santa in person when he handed me a gift, and although I later realized it was just a costume, were we not all once children who believed he was real?19 Whether Santa was real or not, the love, warmth, and compassion I felt in that moment stayed with me forever.20

Teenage Years

During my teenage years, life shifted in ways I could not have anticipated, presenting obstacles, lessons, and deep self-reflection.1 I often felt left behind, from not knowing how to clean myself after using the toilet, tie my own shoes, write in cursive, or take medicine, but I realized that our weaknesses build our strengths.2 The practice of doing these things, even with fear, forged courage within me, and I reminded myself that it was only unfamiliarity, not incapability, with innocence meaning we did not know how, but over time, we figured things out like debugging a program.3 In high school, I was the underdog smart, and often lazy, but when the situation called for it, I shone as the cleverest, earning the nickname “lazy clever boy”.4 During investigatory projects, I was always assigned a leader by my teacher because she knew my potential, even if I was the laziest in the group.5 We created the best investigatory project: malunggay oil and a natural Vicks inhaler without its chemical contents.6 In those years, I was also a playboy, managing the attention of two girls, being a two-timer, which boosted my ego but taught me lessons about responsibility and love.7 Impulsiveness and neglect caused me to lose my class rank, though I excelled in other strengths.8 I realized life is not only about school ranking but about real-life application, earning the title “siga na matalino”.9 I was also a bully, reflecting the bullying I experienced at home, yet I did not enjoy it.10 Ironically, at home, I was a scaredy-cat, terrified of eerie sounds at night or after watching scary movies, a stark contrast to the “siga na matalino” persona I carried at school.11 I had friends who encouraged rebellion, and I often felt like Will Hunting navigating life with intelligence and risk.12 Life in high school went by like skipping frames in an editing software, and I barely cared about schoolwork or requirements.13 I experienced my first heartbreak with a girl I thought I loved, a situationship in which she cheated with my best friend.14 I realized I was accountable for myself, even though she worsened a part of my story.15 The pandemic arrived, which worsened life but also brought families together.16 I returned to my smart self and finished high school with Awards of Excellence without shortcuts.17 Senior high school went by like lightning, and I met a girl who became my first true love, my best friend since Kindergarten 2, my rose in a whole garden of roses, perfectly imperfect, where love, time, and proximity made her unique, and I considered her my true love.18 When I met her, I felt like The Little Prince in Antoine de Saint-Exupéry’s novella, discovering a flower that belonged only to me.19 We graduated with Awards of Excellence together, thanks to shared hard work.20

College Years

Starting college felt like stepping into a new rhythm of life, where each day brought tests, discoveries, and small victories, a place where I constantly strive for good grades, feeling like a rat running endlessly on a wheel.1 I promised myself that once I truly understood who I was, I would break free from the cycle and create the life I desired, because I know I am capable.2 I am a 2nd year Bachelor of Science in Information Technology student at the University of the East, Manila, under the College of Computer Studies and Systems Department, and a Tan Yan Kee scholar.3 Life as an adult has shown me that time moves fast, responsibilities increase, and happiness is not guaranteed.4 I became cold and emotionless at times, realizing that adulthood can be heavy and unforgiving.5 College life became emotionally centered on my breakup and endless sleepless nights, testing both my resolve and patience, and I became a perfectionist, chasing excellence to shield myself from failure.6 I stumbled in my first year, learning that mistakes are part of growth.7 Marcus Aurelius’ Meditations inspired me to practice Stoicism, reminding me to embrace amor fati — to love my fate — and accept life as it comes.8 I also meditate on memento mori, remembering that life is finite and each moment must be lived fully.9 I learned to control what I can, remain calm under pressure, and not let external events dictate my emotions.10 I developed my own methods to survive college life, focusing on personal growth over comparison with others.11 I realized that discomfort and challenges are opportunities to strengthen character.12 I discovered that small victories over fear and procrastination are just as important as high grades.13 College taught me to embrace responsibility, discipline, and patience as virtues.14 Even with overwhelming tasks, I remind myself that endurance builds resilience.15 I see opportunities for growth in every setback, understanding that fate shapes us and our responses matter more than events themselves.16 I cherish fleeting joys, like laughter with friends or quiet reflection at night.17 Each day is a lesson in Stoicism, self-reliance, and perseverance.18 Despite the hardships, I believe that I am forming a strong foundation for my future.19 I conclude with the words of Marcus Aurelius: “The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way.”20
